What is ADHD?

Attention-deficit hyperactivity disorder (ADHD) is a neurodevelopmental disorder that affects both children and adults. It causes symptoms of inattention, hyperactivity, and impulsivity. ADHD symptoms can be mild, moderate, or severe, and they may affect many areas of life, such as school, work, and relationships.

Symptoms of ADHD

Inattention Symptoms:

  • Becomes easily distracted by unrelated stimuli or thoughts.
  • Does not follow through with instructions and fails to finish tasks.
  • Has difficulty organizing tasks and activities.
  • Frequently loses items needed for tasks and activities (e.g., toys, homework, tools).
  • Forgetful in daily activities.

Hyperactivity and Impulsivity Symptoms:

  • Fidgets or squirms when seated.
  • Has difficulty remaining seated in situations where it is expected.
  • Feels restless or talks excessively.
  • Interrupts others or has difficulty waiting for a turn.
  • Engages in risky or impulsive behaviors without considering the consequences.

Causes of ADHD

The exact cause of ADHD is unknown, but several factors are believed to contribute:

  • Genetics: ADHD tends to run in families, indicating a genetic component.
  • Brain structure and function: Differences in brain structure and activity, particularly in areas involved in attention and impulse control, are thought to contribute to ADHD.
  • Environmental factors: Exposure to environmental toxins (e.g., lead), alcohol or tobacco use during pregnancy, premature birth, or low birth weight may increase the risk of developing ADHD.
  • Neurological conditions: Co-existing conditions like learning disabilities or sleep disturbances may exacerbate ADHD symptoms.

Diagnosis of ADHD

ADHD is diagnosed by a healthcare provider, such as a pediatrician, psychiatrist, or psychologist. The diagnosis involves a comprehensive evaluation that includes:

  • Behavioral assessments: Input from teachers, parents, and others who interact with the individual.
  • Medical evaluation: Rule out other conditions that may be causing symptoms.
  • Psychological tests: To assess cognitive and behavioral function.

The diagnosis is based on specific criteria from the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (DSM-5).

Treatment for ADHD

There are various treatment options for ADHD, including:

Medications:

  • Stimulants: The most common medications for ADHD, including methylphenidate (Ritalin) and amphetamines (Adderall), which help improve focus and control impulses.
  • Non-stimulants: Medications like atomoxetine (Strattera) that may be prescribed if stimulants are not effective or cause side effects.

Therapy:

  • Behavioral therapy: Helps individuals with ADHD develop skills to manage their behavior and cope with challenges.
  • Cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT): Helps patients change negative thought patterns that may contribute to poor behavior or impulsivity.
  • Parenting skills training: Helps parents manage ADHD behaviors at home through structured routines and consistent rewards or consequences.

Management of ADHD

Effective management of ADHD typically includes a combination of treatment strategies:

  • Structure and Routine: Creating a predictable and organized environment for individuals with ADHD can help manage symptoms.
  • Supportive School Programs: Schools may provide accommodations, such as extended time for tests or preferential seating, to help children with ADHD succeed.
  • Workplace Adjustments: Adults with ADHD may benefit from flexible work hours or tasks that minimize distractions.
  • Healthy Lifestyle: Regular exercise, a balanced diet, and adequate sleep are essential for individuals with ADHD to maintain focus and overall health.
